## Detailed Framework Findings

### 1. CURIOSITY GAP (Loewenstein 1994)

**What it is:** George Loewenstein's information-gap theory proposes that curiosity arises when people perceive a gap between what they know and what they want to know. This gap creates an aversive state — an "information itch" — that can only be resolved by acquiring the missing knowledge.

**Who proved it:** George Loewenstein, Herbert A. Simon Professor of Economics and Psychology at Carnegie Mellon University. Published "The Psychology of Curiosity: A Review and Reinterpretation" in *Psychological Bulletin* (1994). Loewenstein is Sigmund Freud's great-grandson and a co-founder of behavioral economics.

**Empirical evidence:**
- Loewenstein's 1994 review synthesized decades of curiosity research into the information-gap framework
- Upworthy (2012-2014) weaponized this: editors wrote 25+ headline variants per article, A/B testing curiosity-gap patterns. At peak, 100M+ monthly uniques
- Morning Brew grew to 4M+ subscribers using curiosity-driven subject lines in email
- BuzzFeed's listicle format ("17 Things You Won't Believe...") is a curiosity-gap derivative

**Carousel application:**
- **Slide 1 hook patterns:** "The #1 reason your AI costs are 3x what they should be" / "We cut inference costs 68% — the method surprised us" / "Stop. Your GPU fleet is bleeding money. Here's proof."
- **Best headline patterns:** Number + outcome + knowledge gap. E.g., "3 config changes that cut our Azure AI bill from $3K to $970/day"
- **Key rule:** Reveal enough to create the gap, not enough to close it. The hook slide promises; the CTA slide delivers the resolution path.

### 2. ZEIGARNIK EFFECT (1927)

**What it is:** People remember unfinished or interrupted tasks better than completed ones. An incomplete task creates a "task-specific tension" that keeps the content cognitively accessible.

**Who proved it:** Bluma Zeigarnik, Lithuanian-Soviet psychologist, published 1927 in *Psychologische Forschung*. Inspired by Kurt Lewin's observation that a waiter remembered unpaid orders but forgot paid ones.

**Empirical evidence:**
- Zeigarnik's original experiments showed superior recall for interrupted vs completed tasks
- Lewin's field theory: started tasks establish tension that improves cognitive accessibility; tension relieves upon completion
- SaaS application: "Your profile is 64% complete" messages increase completion rates 20-40%
- Netflix cliffhangers, Duanju format fiction, and serialized content all exploit this effect
- **Caveat:** A 2025 systematic review/meta-analysis found the memory advantage lacks universal validity, though the Ovsiankina effect (urge to resume tasks) is robust

**Carousel application:**
- Each slide ends mid-insight — "But the real savings came from..." → swipe to next
- Multi-part series ("Part 1 of 3: The Problem") creates return-visit tension
- Progress indicators ("Slide 4/10") invoke the completion-urge variant
- "Swipe to see the result →" callouts on data slides

### 3. IKEA EFFECT (Norton/Mochon/Ariely 2011)

**What it is:** Consumers place disproportionately high value on products they partially created. Labor leads to love — even constructing a standardized bureau leads people to overvalue their creations.

**Who proved it:** Michael I. Norton (Harvard Business School), Daniel Mochon (Yale), Dan Ariely (Duke). Published in *Journal of Consumer Psychology* (2011).

**Empirical evidence:**
- Study 1: Self-assemblers paid 63% more for identical IKEA furniture vs pre-assembled
- Study 2: Origami builders valued their creations at 5x what non-builders would pay — AND expected others to value them equally
- Study 3: Complete assembly → higher valuation than partial assembly (labor must result in completion)
- Roots in Festinger's effort justification (1957) and Bandura's self-efficacy (1977)
- Build-a-Bear, "haycations" as commercial applications

**Carousel application:**
- **Interactive assessment slides:** "Which tier are you? A) Overspending B) Optimized C) Cost-Leader" — reader self-categorizes, investing cognitive labor
- **Calculator prompts:** "Multiply your daily GPU hours × $X — that's what you're losing"
- **Fill-in-the-blank:** "My team's AI inference cost per day is $____" mental exercise
- Key: Participation must feel completable (incomplete IKEA builds killed the effect)

### 4. VON RESTORFF ISOLATION EFFECT (1933)

**What it is:** When multiple homogeneous stimuli are presented, the stimulus that differs from the rest is more likely to be remembered. Distinctiveness drives recall.

**Who proved it:** Hedwig von Restorff, German psychiatrist and pediatrician, published 1933. Used the "isolation paradigm" — a distinctive item in a list of similar items.

**Empirical evidence:**
- Distinctiveness can be generated through size, shape, color, spacing, underlining, or semantic category
- Karis/Fabiani/Donchin (1984): greater attentional engagement with isolated items → significantly better recall
- ERP neuroimaging: isolated items produce larger P300 amplitude, predicting higher recall likelihood
- Elhalal/Davelaar/Usher (2014): effect correlates with fluid intelligence
- Parker/Wilding/Akerman (1998): frontal and perirhinal cortex lesions reduce the effect

**Carousel application:**
- **"One Big Number" slide:** A single oversized stat ("68%") on a contrasting background, interrupting a sequence of text-heavy slides
- **Bold quote cards:** A pull-quote in a completely different visual treatment (dark background among light slides)
- **Color disruption:** Slide 4 in a different palette than slides 1-3 and 5-7
- **The "pattern interrupt" slide:** After 3 how-to slides, insert a provocative question or surprising data point

### 5. PROCESSING FLUENCY (Reber/Schwarz/Winkielman 2004)

**What it is:** The subjective ease with which information is processed. Higher fluency → more favorable evaluations across truth, attractiveness, familiarity, and confidence dimensions.

**Who proved it:** Rolf Reber, Norbert Schwarz, Piotr Winkielman — published the "Processing Fluency Theory of Aesthetic Pleasure" (2004). Built on Jacoby & Dallas (1981), Whittlesea (1993), and Topolinski (2014).

**Empirical evidence:**
- Cleaner fonts → statements judged more truthful (illusory truth effect)
- Rhyming statements rated as more accurate than non-rhyming equivalents
- Repeated exposure → fluent processing → liking (mere exposure effect)
- Fluent stimuli activate zygomaticus major muscle (literally make people smile) — Winkielman 2001
- Product design: unusual forms processed disfluently → viewed less favorably
- Too much visual disorder overwhelms; moderate incongruity is optimal

**Carousel application:**
- **Visual-first > text-heavy:** 3-5 words per slide + supporting visual = high fluency. Walls of text = disfluent = judged less true
- **Consistent design system:** Same fonts, colors, layout across slides reduces processing load
- **Whitespace is credibility:** Generous margins and breathing room = higher perceived expertise
- **Implication:** Visual carousel slides are inherently more fluent (and thus more trusted) than text-heavy blog excerpts

### 6. AIDA (Lewis 1898)

**What it is:** Attention → Interest → Desire → Action. The foundational hierarchy-of-effects model for persuasion. Consumers move through cognitive (thinking) and affective (feeling) stages before behavioral (doing) stages.

**Who proved it:** E. St. Elmo Lewis, American advertising pioneer, first articulated 1898. The most widely applied hierarchy of effects model for 125+ years. Variants include AIDAS (adding Satisfaction) and AISDALSLove.

**Empirical evidence:**
- Foundational to purchase funnel theory (marketing funnel)
- Criticized for linear assumption — Vakratsas & Ambler literature review found limited empirical support for strict hierarchy
- Modern integrative models acknowledge dual-pathway (cognitive + affective simultaneously)
- Despite criticism, remains the organizing framework for virtually all advertising and content marketing

**Carousel slide mapping:**
| AIDA Stage | Slide Position | Slide Type | Example |
|------------|---------------|------------|---------|
| **Attention** | Slide 1 | Hook/Pattern Interrupt | "Your AI bill is 3x what it should be" |
| **Interest** | Slides 2-3 | Problem + Data | "Here's the cost breakdown most teams miss" |
| **Desire** | Slides 4-6 | Solution + Proof + Social Proof | "How Team X cut costs 68% in 2 weeks" |
| **Action** | Final Slide | CTA | "Save this. Read the full guide. Link in comments." |

### 7. HEATH SUCCESs (Made to Stick, 2007)

**What it is:** The 6 principles that make ideas "sticky" — Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories.

**Who proved it:** Chip Heath (Stanford GSB professor of organizational behavior) and Dan Heath (former Harvard researcher). *Made to Stick* was a NYT/WSJ bestseller with 24-month BusinessWeek listing.

**Scoring AI cost optimization content against SUCCESs:**

| Principle | Score (1-5) | How to Apply |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Simple** | 5 | "You're overpaying 3x for AI inference" — one core message |
| **Unexpected** | 4 | "The fix isn't more GPUs — it's fewer, configured right" — violates expectation |
| **Concrete** | 5 | "$3K/day → $970/day" — specific numbers, not "significant savings" |
| **Credible** | 4 | Real Azure metrics, named tools (KAITO, vLLM), author credentials |
| **Emotional** | 3 | Fear of waste ("Your CFO will ask about this"); aspiration ("be the engineer who saved $750K/yr") |
| **Stories** | 3 | "When we onboarded our first AKS cluster..." — narrative thread needed |

**Improvement areas:** Emotional and Stories are typically weakest in technical content. Add: "The engineer who showed their CFO this slide got promoted" (story) or "That $2K/day waste? It's someone's salary" (emotion).

### 8. CIALDINI'S 7 PRINCIPLES OF INFLUENCE (1984/2016)

**What it is:** The 7 principles by which humans are persuaded: Reciprocity, Commitment/Consistency, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, Scarcity, Unity.

**Who proved it:** Robert Cialdini, Regents' Professor Emeritus at Arizona State University. *Influence* (1984) sold 5M+ copies in 41 languages; Fortune "75 Smartest Business Books." Elected to National Academy of Sciences 2019. 7th principle (Unity) added in *Pre-Suasion* (2016).

**Highest-impact principles for technical blog traffic (ranked):**

| Rank | Principle | Application | Impact Level |
|------|-----------|------------|-------------|
| 1 | **Authority** | Author credentials, data citations, named tools, Microsoft/Azure references | ★★★★★ |
| 2 | **Social Proof** | "10K teams use this pattern", "trending on r/azure", reader count signals | ★★★★☆ |
| 3 | **Scarcity** | "Before this deprecation hits", "limited-time GPU pricing", "most teams don't know this yet" | ★★★★☆ |
| 4 | **Reciprocity** | Free calculator/template/config file; "Here's the exact Helm chart" — give value first | ★★★☆☆ |
| 5 | **Commitment** | "Save this for later" → micro-commitment → return visit → follow | ★★★☆☆ |
| 6 | **Liking** | Conversational tone, self-deprecating humor, "I made this mistake too" | ★★☆☆☆ |
| 7 | **Unity** | "Fellow platform engineers" — tribal identity | ★★☆☆☆ |

### 9. DUAL-CODING THEORY (Paivio 1971)

**What it is:** The mind processes information along two channels — verbal and nonverbal/imagery. Information encoded in both channels has a retrieval advantage.

**Who proved it:** Allan Paivio, University of Western Ontario, late 1960s. Based on the picture superiority effect. Corroborated by Baddeley's working memory model (visuospatial sketchpad + phonological loop).

**Empirical evidence:**
- Paivio's experiments: images recalled better than words; sequential order recalled better for words
- Shepard (1967): 98.5% recognition rate for pictures (vs ~90% for words)
- Concrete words recalled ~2x better than abstract words (dual-coded naturally)
- Multimedia presentations using both channels → better recall at later testing
- Lee Brooks experiment: visual tasks interfered with visual responses; verbal tasks interfered with verbal responses — confirming separate channels

**Carousel application — why carousels outperform text walls:**
- Blog post = primarily verbal channel (single encoding)
- Carousel slide = image + text label = dual encoding (two retrieval paths)
- Each slide is a "picture" in memory, not a paragraph — this is the fundamental advantage
- Concrete numbers + visual charts = maximum dual-coding benefit
- **Quantified impact:** Dual-coded information recalled at roughly 2x the rate of single-coded

### 10. PEAK-END RULE (Kahneman)

**What it is:** People judge an experience largely based on how they felt at its peak (most intense moment) and at its end, rather than the sum/average of every moment.

**Who proved it:** Barbara Fredrickson and Daniel Kahneman, "snapshot model of remembered utility." Kahneman won Nobel Prize in Economics 2002.

**Empirical evidence:**
- Cold water experiment (Kahneman et al. 1993): Subjects chose longer painful trial (60s + 30s at slightly warmer temp) over shorter one (60s) — better ending → better remembered utility
- Colonoscopy study (Redelmeier/Kahneman 1996): Patients judged discomfort based on worst + final moments, regardless of duration
- Extended colonoscopy study: Leaving scope in 3 extra minutes (uncomfortable but not painful) → patients rated procedure less unpleasant AND returned more for follow-ups
- Duration neglect: Length of experience barely matters; peak + end dominate

**Carousel application:**
- **Slide 1 (hook) = peak moment candidate:** The most emotionally intense, surprising, or provocative statement. This is your "peak."
- **Final slide (CTA) = end moment:** The last impression determines save/share behavior. A weak CTA = weak remembered experience. A strong CTA with clear value = positive end.
- **Implication:** Invest 50%+ of design effort on slide 1 and final slide. Middle slides matter less to overall experience evaluation.
- **Save behavior correlation:** LinkedIn's "save" function is triggered by remembered utility — dominated by peak (hook) and end (CTA). A carousel with a great hook and great CTA but mediocre middle will outperform one with uniformly "good" slides.

### 11. CHIP HEATH "CONCRETE LANGUAGE" (CLUE)

**What it is:** Abstract concepts must be anchored to concrete, sensory language to be understood and remembered. The "Concrete" principle from Made to Stick, supported by dual-coding research showing concrete words are recalled ~2x better than abstract words.

**Who proved it:** Chip & Dan Heath, *Made to Stick* (2007), Chapter 3 "Concrete." Builds on Paivio's dual-coding theory and decades of memory research on concrete vs abstract word recall.

**Application — the number-anchoring technique:**
| Abstract (Weak) | Concrete (Strong) | Why It Works |
|-----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| "Significant cost reduction" | "$3K/day → $970/day" | Specific numbers create mental images |
| "Improved efficiency" | "47% GPU utilization → 89%" | Before/after creates narrative arc |
| "Faster deployment" | "From 3-day rollout to 47 minutes" | Time specificity is sensory |
| "Many teams benefit" | "Adopted by 847 engineering teams at Microsoft" | Specificity implies authority + credibility |
| "AI costs are high" | "Your AI inference bill is someone's annual salary" | Emotional anchor + concrete scale |

**Carousel application:**
- Every slide needs ONE concrete anchor (number, comparison, named entity)
- "$3K/day → $970/day" works because: Simple ✓, Concrete ✓, Unexpected ✓ (3 SUCCESs principles simultaneously)
- Use the "Sinatra Test" (Heath): If you can make it there, you can make it anywhere. One undeniable proof point that carries all credibility.

### 12. OGILVY "BIG IDEA"

**What it is:** Every great advertising campaign is built on one Big Idea. Without a Big Idea, the ad will "pass like a ship in the night." The Big Idea must be research-backed and capable of enduring for years.

**Who proved it:** David Ogilvy (1911-1999), founder of Ogilvy & Mather, "Father of Advertising." Trained at Gallup's research organization. Famous campaigns: Rolls-Royce ("At 60 miles an hour, the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock"), Dove soap, Hathaway shirts.

**Key Ogilvy principles for content:**
- "The consumer is not a moron, she's your wife" — respect intelligence
- "You cannot bore people into buying your product" — every element must earn attention
- Research-driven: Ogilvy attributed success to meticulous Gallup-trained research into consumer habits
- The Big Idea test: "Does it make me gasp when I first see it? Do I wish I had thought of it? Is it unique? Does it fit the strategy to perfection? Could it be used for 30 years?"

**Carousel application — One Big Idea per carousel:**
- The entire 7-10 slide deck distills to ONE takeaway
- For AI cost optimization: **"You're overpaying for AI inference by 3x — here's the fix"**
- Every slide reinforces this single idea from different angles (data, story, how-to, social proof)
- If a slide doesn't reinforce the Big Idea, cut it
- Test: Can someone who saw only slide 1 and the final slide articulate the Big Idea? If yes, it works.

### Historical Visual-First Thought Leaders

**Edward Tufte (b. 1942)**
- Yale Professor Emeritus of Political Science, Statistics, and Computer Science
- Self-published *The Visual Display of Quantitative Information* (1982) with a second mortgage — became landmark text
- Coined "chartjunk" to denounce decorative non-informative elements
- Key concepts: data-ink ratio, data density, lie factor, small multiples
- Core principle: "Above all else, show the data" — maximise information density while minimising non-data ink
- Appointed to Obama's Recovery Independent Advisory Panel (2010)
- **Application:** Tufte's data-ink ratio validates minimal slide design. Every pixel should encode data or meaning. Remove all chartjunk.

**Dan Roam**
- Author of *The Back of the Napkin: Solving Problems and Selling Ideas with Pictures* (2008)
- Popularized visual problem-solving in business contexts
- Framework: "6 ways of seeing" (who/what, how much, where, when, how, why) → each maps to a visual type
- Core thesis: Any problem can be clarified and solved with a simple picture
- **Application:** Validates the "one visual per slide" principle. Complex AI cost optimization → simple napkin-sketch clarity.

**Bill Bernbach (1911-1982)**
- Co-founder of Doyle Dane Bernbach (DDB), pioneer of advertising's Creative Revolution (1960s-70s)
- First to combine copywriters and art directors into two-person teams (now industry standard)
- Volkswagen "Think Small" and "Lemon" campaigns — visual simplicity + single idea
- Famous letter to Grey Advertising: "Advertising is fundamentally persuasion and persuasion happens to be not a science, but an art"
- **Application:** Bernbach's art-copy integration is the template for carousel slides: one visual + one line of copy per slide. His "Think Small" is the original visual-first campaign.

**David Ogilvy (1911-1999)**
- "Father of Advertising," founded Ogilvy & Mather
- Gallup-trained research methodology applied to advertising
- Rolls-Royce ad: one Big Idea, one headline, research-backed claim
- Principle: "The customer is not a moron, she's your wife"
- **Application:** Ogilvy's Big Idea principle = one carousel = one Big Idea. His research obsession = every claim in a slide must have data backing.

### LinkedIn/X "Save" Behavior and Authority Building Correlation

**LinkedIn save mechanics:**
- "Save" is LinkedIn's strongest engagement signal for content quality
- Saved content enters a personal library, creating return visits and extended distribution
- LinkedIn algorithm interprets saves as high-value engagement (weighted higher than likes, possibly equal to comments)
- Carousel/document format generates disproportionate saves because: (a) reference material is save-worthy, (b) multi-slide format implies depth, (c) visual design signals effort/authority

**Authority building correlation:**
- **Save → Return → Follow cycle:** Users who save content are more likely to return to the author's profile and follow. Saves are a leading indicator of follower growth.
- **Peak-End Rule connection:** The decision to save occurs at the END of the carousel (after swiping through). The final slide (CTA) directly triggers or fails to trigger save behavior.
- **Reciprocity principle (Cialdini):** High-value free content triggers reciprocity → save, share, follow, comment
- **Authority principle (Cialdini):** Consistent publication of data-backed visual content builds perceived expertise → authority → organic reach amplification
- **Social Proof compounding:** Each saved/shared post creates visible social proof ("Liked by [industry leader]") → authority snowball effect

**X/Twitter save (bookmark) mechanics:**
- Bookmarks are private — no social proof signal
- However, bookmark rate correlates with content quality for recommendation algorithm
- Thread format (X's carousel equivalent) generates higher bookmark rate than single tweets
- Visual threads (image per tweet) outperform text-only threads in bookmark rate

## SCORING RUBRIC: Evaluating Distilled Posts Across All Psychology Levers

Rate each slide or carousel as a whole on a 1-5 scale per framework:

| Framework | 1 (Absent) | 3 (Present) | 5 (Optimized) | Weight |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|----------------|--------|
| **Curiosity Gap** | No knowledge gap created | Mild question raised | Irresistible gap that demands resolution | 15% |
| **Zeigarnik Effect** | Complete info per slide; no tension | Some "continue" cues | Every slide ends mid-insight; series tension | 8% |
| **IKEA Effect** | Passive consumption only | Light interaction prompts | Active self-assessment or calculation | 5% |
| **Von Restorff** | All slides look identical | One slightly different slide | 1-2 deliberately disruptive "pattern interrupt" slides | 10% |
| **Processing Fluency** | Dense text, small fonts, busy layout | Clean with some text-heavy areas | Minimal text, high contrast, generous whitespace, visual-first | 12% |
| **AIDA Mapping** | No persuasion arc | Partial funnel coverage | Clear Attention→Interest→Desire→Action across slide positions | 10% |
| **SUCCESs Score** | Misses most principles | Hits 3-4 of 6 | Scores 4+ on all 6 principles | 12% |
| **Cialdini Principles** | No influence principles | 2-3 principles present | Authority + Social Proof + Scarcity layered throughout | 10% |
| **Dual-Coding** | Text-only or image-only slides | Mixed approach | Every slide has verbal + visual encoding with concrete elements | 8% |
| **Peak-End Rule** | Weak hook, weak CTA | Strong hook OR strong CTA | Both hook and CTA are peak-intensity moments | 15% |
| **Concrete Language** | Abstract, vague claims | Some numbers/specifics | Every slide has a concrete anchor ($, %, time, named entity) | 8% |
| **Big Idea Clarity** | Multiple competing messages | Main idea present but diluted | ONE Big Idea; every slide reinforces it; passes Ogilvy test | 12% |

**Total weighted score interpretation:**
- **4.0-5.0:** Publication-ready, high-conversion potential
- **3.0-3.9:** Solid foundation, optimize weak areas
- **2.0-2.9:** Significant gaps; revisit hook, CTA, and visual design
- **1.0-1.9:** Rebuild needed; start from Big Idea and work outward

**Note:** Weights reflect empirical impact on save/share behavior. Peak-End Rule and Curiosity Gap are weighted highest (15% each) because hook and CTA slides disproportionately drive the save decision (per Kahneman's peak-end research). Big Idea Clarity and Processing Fluency are next (12% each) because message coherence and visual ease determine whether the middle slides are consumed at all.

| Bold visual/number | Von Restorff (isolation) | Giant "$3K" in contrasting color |

| Big Idea teaser | Ogilvy Big Idea | "Here's the fix" (promise) |
| Peak intensity | Peak-End Rule | This must be the most emotionally intense slide |

### Slides 2-3: The Problem/Data
**Primary frameworks:** SUCCESs (Unexpected + Concrete) + Dual-Coding
**Secondary:** AIDA (Interest stage), Concrete Language

| Element | Framework Applied | Example |
|---------|------------------|---------|
| Surprising data point | SUCCESs — Unexpected | "73% of teams never right-size their GPU SKUs" |
| Before/after numbers | Concrete Language (CLUE) | "$3K/day → visual cost breakdown" |
| Chart + text label | Dual-Coding | Bar chart with one-sentence insight |
| Zeigarnik cliffhanger | Zeigarnik Effect | "But the real waste isn't compute — it's..." |

### Slides 4-6: The Solution/Proof
**Primary frameworks:** SUCCESs (Credible + Concrete + Stories) + Cialdini (Authority + Social Proof)
**Secondary:** AIDA (Desire stage), IKEA Effect

| Element | Framework Applied | Example |
|---------|------------------|---------|
| Step-by-step method | SUCCESs — Simple | "3 changes: SKU, autoscale, model selection" |
| Author credentials | Cialdini — Authority | "Azure MVP, 200+ AKS deployments" |
| Named tool/config | SUCCESs — Concrete | "KAITO InferenceWorkspace with vLLM backend" |
| Case study snippet | SUCCESs — Stories | "When Team X deployed this..." |
| Team count | Cialdini — Social Proof | "Used by 847 engineering teams" |
| Interactive prompt | IKEA Effect | "Which tier matches your setup?" |

### Slide 7 (or penultimate): The Pattern Interrupt
**Primary frameworks:** Von Restorff + SUCCESs (Emotional)
**Secondary:** Concrete Language

| Element | Framework Applied | Example |

### Final Slide: The CTA
**Primary frameworks:** Peak-End (end) + Cialdini (Commitment + Reciprocity + Scarcity)

1. **Quantitative LinkedIn carousel save-rate benchmarks by industry vertical** — limited public data from LinkedIn; need first-party analytics or social media tool reports (Hootsuite, Sprout Social)
2. **A/B test results for curiosity-gap vs direct-statement headlines on LinkedIn specifically** — Upworthy data is Facebook-era; LinkedIn audience may respond differently
