## Screen Recording Notes

* App or tool to show: VS Code with this repository open, plus the rendered PNGs as full-frame cuts
* Theme: light or high-contrast neutral so the rendered visuals stay legible after the vertical crop
* Zoom: 150–175% when showing any chat or file content; crop the PNGs vertically, never show a full landscape frame
* Actions to perform:
  1. Open on the `p1-05-pull-quote.png` reframe — snap zoom for the 3-second hook.
  2. Briefly show a blurred AI chat with prompt tricks highlighted to illustrate "cleverest wording."
  3. Vertical pan up `p1-01-staircase.png`, revealing one era at a time in sync with the voiceover.
  4. Hard-cut between the two numbers on `p2-04-stripe-swebench.png` (1,300+ PRs/week, then 12.47% → 76.8%).
  5. Trace the cycle on `p2-01-loop.png`; pause a beat on the verification gate and stop condition.
  6. Reveal `p2-05-first-loop-checklist.png` item-by-item, then cut to the blog CTA title card.
* Editing notes:
  * Keep text overlays to 6 words or fewer.
  * Hard cuts on data points; slow push-ins on the diagrams.
  * Do not show private chat history, account names, tokens, or unpublished credentials.

## Voiceover Script (full, ~75s at natural pace)

You're not getting better at prompting — and that's fine.

The skill didn't get better. It moved. The people shipping real software with agents aren't the ones with the cleverest wording.

It's one staircase, four eras. Prompt engineering: the wording. Context engineering: what the model sees. Harness engineering: the rig around it. Loop engineering: the cycle it runs.

Why now? Generation got cheap, so validation is the bottleneck. Stripe ships 1,300-plus PRs a week with zero human-written code. On a fixed harness, SWE-bench climbed from 12 to 77 percent.

So you stop tuning sentences and start owning the loop: plan, act, observe, verify, correct — with a gate that checks the work and a stop condition that ends it.

Ship one this week. Take a task you babysit line-by-line. Give it four things: a clear goal, the tools to iterate, one feedback signal it can read, and a stop condition.

Full four-era breakdown's in the blog.
